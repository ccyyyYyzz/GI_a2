\begin{abstract}
Dead time makes the flux that maximizes an integrated photon count's
information finite, and hidden recovery-time jitter makes that optimum depend
on the detector rather than on dwell alone.

For a count-only nonparalyzable detector, an exact missing-information identity
shows that uncertainty in the unobserved active time subtracts information from
the complete detector path.

In long windows the retained information depends on hold-time variance, and its
unique optimum obeys $c_v^{2}\rho^{2}(1+2\rho)=1$, yielding a jitter cap that
scales as $c_v^{-2/3}$.

A single global power multiplier, calibrated from a common pre-scan, implements
this detector-specific operating policy without changing the illumination
geometry from pattern to pattern.

In a preregistered paired simulation campaign, ridge tracking improved
fixed-dwell radiometric quality by a median $+1.87$~dB, with 19 of 24 scenes
positive; this power-for-time comparison used $37.1\times$ the incident dose
and $2.6\times$ the detected counts of its matched comparator and is not an
equal-photon-budget comparison.

Against the safe-flux reference, the median elapsed-time speedup was
$19.13\times$, its family-stratified lower bound was $18.33\times$, and 21 of
24 scenes accelerated.

Separate scene-adapted geometry searches remained descriptive: they exposed
feasible headroom under both dose-only and full conditioning constraints but
left the scope and reliability of adaptive geometry unresolved.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Single-pixel imaging (SPI) reconstructs a scene from the responses of one
bucket detector to a sequence of structured illumination
patterns~\cite{duarte2008singlepixel,shapiro2008computational,edgar2019principles}.
When that bucket is a photon-counting detector, every registered event makes it
blind for a recovery time. Operating at a few per cent of the dead-time rate
avoids strong nonlinearity but spends acquisition time; increasing power gains
counts only until dead-time censoring removes more information than the added
arrivals provide. The practical question is therefore not simply whether the
count response can be corrected, but where a real detector should be operated.

For deterministic recovery, the information-optimal load grows as the cube
root of the exposure length~\cite{companion}. Real recovery times fluctuate.
Here we show that hidden hold-time variance produces an extensive information
loss, caps the optimal load, and can make the deterministic operating point
actively harmful. At $\nu=2000$, for example, a hold-time coefficient of
variation $c_v=0.05$ moves the optimum from approximately $22.3$ to $5.7$;
using the deterministic ridge then forfeits about $55\%$ of the scalar count
information (Fig.~\ref{fig:hero}).

The resulting control is deliberately simple: characterize the detector,
maximize its finite-window count-information kernel, and use one global source
multiplier to place a fixed balanced pattern family at that load. A
preregistered simulation campaign tests the policy at fixed dwell and in
elapsed optical time. The policy improves median fixed-dwell quality by
$1.87$~dB and reduces the time to a frozen quality target by a median
$19.13\times$, while explicitly spending $37.1\times$ more incident dose at
the terminal dwell. A separate geometry analysis is retained only to locate
unresolved headroom; it is not the paper's positive claim.

Relative to work on high-flux lidar, count-rate correction, and task-specific
dead-time optima~\cite{rapp2019deadtime,rapp2021highflux,
jorgensen2026deadtime,alvarez2014pileup}, the contribution is a count-only
random-hold law tied to an immediately deployable global power policy and a
paired image-level test. The observation is the integrated count, not event
timestamps, and all image evidence is simulation-based.

\section{Random-hold count information}
\label{sec:random-hold}
Let arrivals be Poisson with rate $\lambda$, and let every registered event
initiate a hidden iid hold $B_i\sim H$, independent of the arrivals and of
$\lambda$, with mean $\tau$ and coefficient of variation
$c_v=\sigma_B/\tau$. Only the integrated count $N_T$ over
$T=\nu\tau$ is observed; the detector-active time $L_T$, state path, and
realized holds remain hidden. The deterministic model is the special case
$c_v=0$.

\begin{theorem}[Missing-information identity]
\label{thm:identity}
For $\theta=\log\lambda$, the complete detector-path information is
$\mathbb{E}[N_T]$, and the information retained by the scalar count is exactly
\begin{equation}
 I_N(\theta;T)=\mathbb{E}[N_T]
 -\lambda^2\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\operatorname{Var}(L_T\mid N_T)\right]
 \label{eq:identity}
\end{equation}
for every finite $T$ and every iid hidden hold law $H$ independent of
$\lambda$.
\end{theorem}

Equation~(\ref{eq:identity}) identifies the loss caused by count-only
observation: uncertainty about how long the detector was active remains after
conditioning on the recorded count. The deterministic detector confines this
uncertainty to the terminal phase, whereas random holds accumulate it throughout
the exposure. The identity requires amendment when holds depend on the load or
scene, are observed by the electronics, are serially correlated, or when the
counter is paralyzable.

\begin{theorem}[Long-window information rate]
\label{thm:rate}
Under standard nonlattice renewal local-limit conditions,
\begin{equation}
 \begin{aligned}
 \frac{I_N(\log\lambda;T)}{\nu}&\longrightarrow J_\infty(\rho,c_v),\\
 J_\infty(\rho,c_v)&=\frac{\rho}{(1+\rho)(1+c_v^2\rho^2)},
 \qquad \rho=\lambda\tau .
 \end{aligned}
 \label{eq:Jinf}
\end{equation}
The limiting loss depends on $H$ through its mean and variance and remains
extensive as $\nu\to\infty$ whenever $c_v>0$.
\end{theorem}

The supplement proves both statements and gives the regularity conditions. In
particular, the long-window result is intended for regular finite-variance iid
hold families; it is not asserted for heavy-tailed, drifting, correlated,
observed, or load-dependent recovery times.

\section{Jitter-capped optimal load}
\label{sec:optimal-load}
\begin{theorem}[Exact jitter-limited optimum]
\label{thm:cubic}
For every fixed $c_v>0$, $J_\infty(\rho,c_v)$ has a unique interior maximum
$\rho_c$ satisfying
\begin{equation}
 c_v^2\rho_c^2(1+2\rho_c)=1,
 \label{eq:cubic}
\end{equation}
with small-jitter expansion
\begin{equation}
 \rho_c=2^{-1/3}c_v^{-2/3}-\tfrac16
 +\frac{2^{1/3}}{36}c_v^{2/3}+O(c_v^{4/3}).
 \label{eq:smallc}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

Thus zero hold-time variance is the critical manifold on which the optimal
load continues to grow with exposure length; every fixed nonzero hidden
variance yields a finite long-window optimum. The exponent pair
$(1/3,-2/3)$ applies within the regular finite-variance iid hidden-hold class,
not to the excluded detector models listed above.

\subsection{Finite-window crossover}
Matching the random-hold loss against the deterministic terminal-boundary loss
in the joint regime $\nu\to\infty$, $c_v\to0$, and
$c_v^2\nu=O(1)$ gives
\begin{equation}
 \rho_*(\nu,c_v)\sim
 (6\nu)^{1/3}(1+12\nu c_v^2)^{-1/3}.
 \label{eq:crossover}
\end{equation}
The deterministic limit recovers the cube-root ridge and the jitter-dominated
limit recovers $2^{-1/3}c_v^{-2/3}$. Equation~(\ref{eq:crossover}) is a
matched-asymptotic prediction, not a universal finite-window theorem; the
supplement states the uniform expansion under which it follows.

\subsection{Numerical verification}
Predictions were registered before the refined simulations. At $\nu=2000$,
fresh points at $c_v=0.02$ and $0.2$ agreed with their predicted peaks to
$3\%$ and $6\%$, respectively. A continuous-peak run and an independent
replication then gave a pooled log--log slope of $-0.658$, within $1.3\%$ of
$-2/3$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:jitter-validation}). Full grids, run sizes, the two-run
peak table, and the recorded refutation of an intermediate multiplier
hypothesis are reported in the supplement.

\section{Global power-control policy}
\label{sec:power-policy}
For a detector with measured recovery law $H$, define the operating target as
the smallest maximizer of its finite-window count-information kernel over the
admissible load interval,
\begin{equation}
 \rhoR(\nu,H)=\min\arg\max_{\rho\in\mathcal{R}_{\rm adm}(\nu)}
 J_H(\rho,\nu).
 \label{eq:production-ridge}
\end{equation}
A common low-cost pre-scan estimates mean bucket brightness. One global source
multiplier per dwell then places the mean load at $\rhoR$, followed by a single
global safety clip; there is no per-pattern servo. The imaging campaign uses
the deterministic recovery kernel to isolate this operating-point effect, for
which $\rhoR(2000)=22.25$. In a detector with nonzero measured jitter, the same
policy replaces that target with the jitter-capped maximizer.

The resource interpretation is essential. At fixed dwell, ridge tracking is a
power-for-time policy: it deliberately increases incident dose to obtain more
information before the acquisition deadline. Under a hard incident-photon
budget, by contrast, the relevant scalar efficiency is information per incident
load, which favors lower operating points. The campaign therefore reports
incident dose and detected counts beside every quality or speed benefit and
makes no equal-photon-budget claim.

Illumination geometry is held fixed in the paired operating-point tests. A
separate local design-measure analysis asks whether scene-adapted patterns can
improve a log-determinant information surrogate under dose and conditioning
constraints. Its generalized KKT system, dictionaries, guards, rounding, and
solver checks are given in the supplement; because that analysis is descriptive,
it is used only to expose remaining geometry headroom.

\section{Preregistered imaging campaign}
\label{sec:campaign}
The campaign uses 24 fresh simulated scenes from six structural families, five
paired measurement seeds, and nine dwell budgets
$\nu\in\{5,10,20,50,100,200,500,1000,2000\}$. Every operating-point arm uses
the same balanced SCAT32 geometry, the same common pre-scan, the same main
pattern multiset, and the same RQL reconstruction. The safe reference operates
at mean load $0.05$, the fixed-dwell comparator at $0.60$, and the ridge arm
uses the dwell-dependent global multiplier of Eq.~(\ref{eq:production-ridge}).
Complete exposure accounting, scene generation, context arms, and immutable
manifests are in the supplement.

The fixed-dwell primary compares ridge control with the $0.60$ comparator at
$\nu=2000$. It passes when the median paired radiometric-PSNR gain is at least
$1$~dB, its family-stratified bootstrap lower bound exceeds zero, and at least
18 of 24 scenes are positive; numerical or guard failures remain in the cohort
as nonpositive. The elapsed-time secondary compares ridge control with the
$0.05$ safe reference using frozen equal-weight isotonic fits, a Q90 target,
and a predeclared censoring taxonomy. It passes when median
$S_{\mathrm{gate}}\ge3$, the bootstrap lower bound exceeds one, and at least
18 of 24 scenes have $S_{\mathrm{gate}}>1$. The exact endpoint names and full
analysis specification are preserved in the supplement; the operating-point
endpoint is \texttt{RIDGE\_OPERATING\_PASS}.

The separate geometry analysis assigns each of 480 full-conditioning cells one
of three descriptive statuses: near-optimality certified, a feasible design
with improvement above the frozen bar found, or numerically unresolved. It has
no PASS/FAIL endpoint. The status definitions, full per-cell fields, compute
protocol, and development branch are reported in the supplement.

\subsection{Analysis correction and provenance}
After the campaign was unblinded, an independent
peer audit found that the frozen scoring implementation did not reproduce three
parts of the preregistered analysis. It placed the Q90 crossings on
$\log(\nu\rho)$ rather than on the frozen elapsed optical time
$\log T_{\mathrm{opt}}$; it resampled family labels rather than retaining all
six fixed strata with paired within-image seed resampling; and it used a
nonstandard isotonic implementation while omitting the frozen censoring
taxonomy. The immutable tag, raw cells, and original analyzer outputs were
preserved unchanged. A dated correction artifact recomputed the endpoints from
the same frozen raw cells using the preregistered axis and machinery; the
promoted independent implementation and an in-project rerun agreed on all 18
audited numerical outputs with no deep differences. Scientific claims and tables
use the spec-conformant corrected results, while the original outputs remain
available in the provenance record. The source-of-record bundle is
\texttt{results/round63\_m1/CORRECTION\_2026-07-19/}, specifically
\texttt{M1\_VERDICTS\_SPEC\_CORRECTED\_R19.json} and
\texttt{ANALYSIS\_CORRECTION\_DISCLOSURE.md}.

\section{Imaging results}
\label{sec:results}
\subsection{Fixed-dwell quality and elapsed-time speed}
The preregistered operating-point endpoint passed: at $\nu=2000$, ridge
control improved median fixed-dwell radiometric quality over the matched
$0.60$ comparator by $+1.87$~dB, with a family-stratified lower bound of
$1.41$~dB and 19 of 24 scenes positive. The same operating point used
$37.1\times$ the incident dose and $2.6\times$ the detected counts. Using
the preregistered elapsed optical-time coordinate,
$T_{\mathrm{opt}}=M_{\mathrm{total}}\nu\tau$, and the frozen
Q90/PAVA/censoring procedure, the ridge time-to-quality endpoint passed: the
median conservative speed ratio was $19.13\times$, the family-stratified
nested-bootstrap 2.5th-percentile bound was $18.33\times$, and 21 of 24
scenes had $S_{\mathrm{gate}}>1$. These are fixed-dwell and elapsed-time
benefits bought with power, not photon-efficiency gains.

(The post-unblinding implementation-to-specification correction and its
provenance are reported in the preceding ``Analysis correction and
provenance'' subsection.)

The fixed-dwell response is scene dependent. The median family gain ranges
from $+6.37$~dB for maze-like scenes to $-4.18$~dB for contour scenes, and
the per-scene gain correlates with measured bucket contrast at $+0.64$.
No load, ceiling, or reconstruction guard explains the negative cases; they
instead delimit where one global multiplier is insufficiently aligned with
scene structure. The complete per-family and per-scene tables are in the
supplement. A post-hoc incident-exposure version of the Q90 analysis gave a
median ratio $0.279$ and no verdict, confirming that the elapsed-time speedup
is not an incident-photon saving.

\section{Geometry-design headroom}
\label{sec:geometry-headroom}
Scene-adapted geometry retained measurable headroom in two deliberately
separate searches. A development-only dose-uniform construction improved the
local determinant over balanced SCAT32 in all 12 family--anchor cells, giving
D-efficiency lower bounds from $1.64\times$ to $2.51\times$. Full-conditioning
feasible searches on fresh campaign cells also found concrete designs with
larger local information. These are existence results for a local information
surrogate, not confirmatory image endpoints or estimates of a population
optimum.

The full-conditioning analysis remained descriptive: 0 of 480 cells certified
SCAT32 near-optimality, 299 produced feasible counterexamples, and 181 were
numerically unresolved. It therefore establishes neither SCAT32
near-optimality nor a reliable adaptive replacement. The retired adaptive path,
development replication table, status distribution, conic construction,
solver protocol, and all caveats appear in the supplement.

\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
The central practical result is a detector-specific operating rule. Hidden
hold-time variation changes the information optimum from a dwell-dependent
deterministic ridge to a finite jitter cap, and a single global power control
can track that optimum without per-pattern feedback. In the paired simulations,
the rule delivered a large elapsed-time benefit and a smaller, strongly
scene-dependent fixed-dwell benefit. Their resource cost is equally central:
the policy converts available incident power into shorter dwell and is not a
photon-budget advantage.

Geometry adaptation remains an open second axis. Feasible designs demonstrate
that balanced illumination is not known to exhaust the available local
information, but the descriptive analysis does not identify a stable adaptive
policy or validate image-level gains. A future method must jointly control
geometry, conditioning, and the jitter-limited scalar operating point, then be
tested on fresh scenes and hardware.

\subsection{Limitations}
First, the terminal ridge policy requires substantial peak and average optical
power: the reported fixed-dwell operating point uses $37.1\times$ the incident
dose of its comparator, so source limits, spatial-light-modulator limits,
laser safety, or sample damage may bind before the detector optimum is reached.
Second, the load target is only as reliable as the recovery-time calibration;
the theory assumes a stable iid hidden-hold law, whereas drift, correlation,
paralyzable behavior, or load-dependent recovery requires a different kernel.
Third, all imaging results are simulation-only. Exact renewal simulation,
paired seeds, and preregistration do not replace optical-bench validation.

\subsection{Immediate bench test}
The count-information law can be tested before a complete imaging system is
built. Time-tagged recovery intervals estimate $c_v$; repeated fixed-dwell
frames acquired during a source-power sweep then trace $J(\rho)$. For
$c_v=0.05$ and $\nu=2000$, the predicted peak is near $\rho=5.7$ rather than
$22$, with roughly half the information lost at the deterministic ridge. A
subsequent SPI experiment can compare the same balanced pattern sequence under
safe and detector-specific global power control while recording incident dose,
detected counts, and elapsed time.
