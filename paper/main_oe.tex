\begin{abstract}
Single-pixel imaging with photon-counting detectors is conventionally run at
low flux so that detector dead time removes few arrivals, but this protection
costs acquisition time. We ask when deliberately entering the nonlinear
dead-time regime improves count-only single-pixel imaging and when it merely
adds saturated photons.

A convex renewal quasi-likelihood (RQL) reconstruction models the integrated
nonparalyzable count without per-scene tuning. Its finite-window Fisher
information has a principal ridge at
$\rho^{*}(\nu)=(6\nu)^{1/3}-2/3+O(\nu^{-1/3})$, while the bucket-energy
contrast of the illumination patterns determines how much of that scalar count
information reaches scene structure.

In a preregistered dense-pattern study, the time-to-quality endpoint was
uninformative and therefore formally negative for every scene; a small but
uniformly positive fixed-dwell benefit remained, with median gain
$+0.28$~dB. Dense multiplexing thus suppressed structural modulation even
when dead-time correction remained accurate.

In a second preregistered study, spatial occupancy varied at equal mean
detector load and equal incident dose across occupancies. The acquisition-speed
primary remained formally negative because its breadth criterion failed,
whereas the fixed-dwell secondary passed with a median $+1.16$~dB gain. The
response across occupancies was nonmonotone, exposing a contrast--conditioning
trade-off rather than a universal optimal sparsity.

Together, the results define a contrast--dead-time operating map: high flux is
useful only when illumination preserves enough bucket modulation for the scene
and the operator remains sufficiently conditioned. Sparse equal-dose operation
also concentrates power into fewer illuminated pixels, so peak irradiance can
bind before the detector limit. All evidence here is simulation-only and
requires optical-bench validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% Para 1 — field-first (Chen style).
Single-pixel imaging (SPI) and computational ghost imaging reconstruct a
scene from the temporal sequence of a single bucket detector's responses
to structured illumination \cite{duarte2008singlepixel,shapiro2008computational,
edgar2019principles}, and have proven particularly attractive where array
sensors are unavailable or photon budgets are severe:\ low-light and
photon-starved imaging, NIR/SWIR sensing, and imaging through scattering
media such as turbid water \cite{hao2025photonlimited,hao2025randommedia,
hao2025complexfield}. In the photon-starved regime the bucket detector is
a photon-counting device --- a single-photon avalanche diode or
photomultiplier --- and image quality is set by how many photons can be
counted within the available acquisition time.

% Para 2 — the pain point: the low-flux convention.
Photon-counting detectors, however, are blind for a dead time $\tau$
after each registered count. The established response is to operate at
low flux --- keeping the dead-time duty $\rho = \lambda\tau$ at the few-percent
level so that losses stay small and a linear correction suffices --- and to
pay for it with acquisition time, since the count rate is then a small
fraction of the detector's $1/\tau$ ceiling. A substantial literature
instead corrects or models the distortion at higher rates:\ dead-time
compensation and high-flux operation in single-photon lidar
\cite{rapp2019deadtime,rapp2021highflux,gupta2019photonflooded,
gupta2019asynchronous}, count-rate corrections in photon-counting
imaging \cite{liu2021photoncounting}, and dead-time-aware estimation
bounds \cite{jorgensen2026deadtime}. These works are
correction-centric or timestamp-based; what remains unmapped is the
operating question for the count-only bucket channel itself:\ at what
flux, for which scenes, does raising the rate into the nonlinear regime
actually help?

% Para 3 — the ridge reframe. Safe novelty wording VERBATIM from round-4/5:
Previous studies established count-rate-dependent CRLB degradation,
task-specific optimal operating fluxes, and high-flux recovery using
time-resolved dead-time models \cite{wang2011pileup,becares2012deadtime,
gupta2019photonflooded,rapp2019deadtime,jorgensen2026deadtime}. Here,
we study a different information bottleneck: a finite exposure from which only
the integrated nonparalyzable count is retained. For this count-only
observation, we derive a principal finite-window Fisher-information ridge and
show that its optimal load obeys a cube-root law, $\rho^{*}(\nu) =
(6\nu)^{1/3} - 2/3 + O(\nu^{-1/3})$, arising from terminal detector-phase
information loss.

% Para 4 — the sparse-pattern prior art + the round-8 SAFE NOVELTY SENTENCE
% (verbatim, frozen). Zhang/Liu/Han keys from refs.bib when built.
Sparse illumination itself is not the claimed contribution of this work.
Prior work has used sparse patterns to reduce detector count rates and
alleviate pile-up \cite{liu2019pileup,han2025sparsehadamard}. Here,
instead, spatial occupancy is varied under equal mean detector load and
equal incident dose, revealing a contrast-controlled boundary between
multiplex-limited and photon-limited high-flux single-pixel imaging.

\section{Physical model and problem statement}
\label{sec:model}
% Frozen content (spec D2.1 §2): non-paralyzable renewal counting, active
% start, exact PMF; dimensionless axes; arrival rates lambda_i = Phi <a_i, x>
% + d; raw-count observation; the exact PMF via the Poisson representation.
A single-pixel detector with dead time $\tau$ observes, for illumination
pattern $a_i$ and scene $x \ge 0$, a Poisson arrival process of rate
$\lambda_i = \Phi\, a_i^{\top} x + d$ during an exposure of duration
$T = \nu\tau$; after each registered count the detector is blind for $\tau$
(non-paralyzable, active start). The recorded observation is the integrated
count $N_i$ alone, with exact law
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  P(N_i \ge m)
    &=F_{\Gamma(m,\lambda_i)}\!\bigl(T-(m-1)\tau\bigr)\\
    &=P\bigl(\mathrm{Pois}(z_m)\ge m\bigr),\\
  z_m&=\lambda_i\bigl(T-(m-1)\tau\bigr).
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:exactpmf}
\end{equation}
The per-slot load $\rho_i = \lambda_i \tau$ is the dead-time duty; the
conventional regime keeps $\rho \lesssim 0.05$.
Illumination ensembles used in this work:\ dense Bernoulli($1/2$)
patterns (Study~1 primary), complementary Hadamard-type pairs charged at
$2M$ physical exposures and gamma-modulated patterns (supplementary
ensemble sensitivity), and the Study-2 fixed-occupancy binary ladder of
Section~\ref{sec:study2}. Because patterns spread instantaneous load,
deployment statements use the per-pattern 95th percentile of $\rho$
rather than its mean; percentile ledgers for every cell are part of the
released evidence bundles.

\section{Renewal quasi-likelihood reconstruction}
\label{sec:rql}
% Frozen: the convex objective; why not full Gaussian (supplement pointer);
% relation of the single-frame root to pre-correction (honesty clause).
We reconstruct by
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  \hat{x}=\arg\min_{x\ge0}\;&\frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M}
  \Bigl[(T-N_i\tau)\lambda_i(x)-N_i\log\lambda_i(x)\Bigr]\\
  &+\lambda_{\mathrm{TV}}\,\mathrm{TV}(x),
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:rql}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_i(x) = \Phi a_i^{\top} x + d$. The data term is convex in
$\lambda$ (and in $x$ through the affine map); its single-measurement
stationary point $\hat\lambda = N/(T - N\tau)$ coincides with the classical
dead-time rate correction, so the estimator's novelty resides not in the
per-measurement formula but in the joint regularized inverse problem and the
operating-point map it enables. At ceiling counts ($N\tau \ge T$) the
objective is unbounded below in $\lambda$ — the true identifiability boundary
of count-only observation; the fraction of ceiling counts is recorded per
cell. A full heteroscedastic Gaussian likelihood fails at high load through a
variance-collapse non-coercivity (Supplement~\S S1).

\subsection{Truth-free regularization selection and descriptive audit}
\label{sec:selection}
% Frozen F1 rule (round-6 analytic rule + round-5 audit). Prereg wording:
The TV weight is set by a frozen analytic score-concentration rule with no
per-scene tuning: $\lambda_{\mathrm{TV}} = c\,\sigma_{g}\sqrt{2\ln n}$ with
global constant $c = 0.50$ frozen before any confirmatory data existed, and
$\sigma_{g} = \Phi\sqrt{\kappa_A\, v_s / M}$ computed from the exact renewal
per-count score variance $v_s$ (no Gaussian approximation; the central limit
theorem is quantitatively untrustworthy at short windows $\nu \le 10$). All
arms are scaled by the identical rule through their own $\kappa_A$.

The measurement audit is descriptive and does not constitute a model-adequacy
test. All audit statistics are excluded from regularization selection,
reconstruction, cell inclusion, and confirmatory pass/fail decisions. The
refit-bootstrap reference distribution is conditional on a smoothed plug-in
scene; its empirical tail ranks are therefore not calibrated model-class
$p$-values for arbitrary scenes and can be anti-conservative in the upper
tail when the true scene is rougher than the plug-in. Moreover, count-only
observations collected at a single operating point cannot generally
distinguish detector mean-response mismatch from radiometric rescaling of the
scene. Detector-mismatch consequences are therefore quantified directly
through the preregistered radiometric metrics in
Section~\ref{sec:mismatch}.

\section{The finite-window count-information ridge}
\label{sec:ridge}
% Ported from paper/THEORY_SECTION_DRAFT.md X.1–X.4 (source of record:
% docs/ROUND63_GPT_ROUND3_DIGEST.md §2). X.5 relation-to-prior-work stays
% \SPH pending the Grönberg full-text sign-off (S5 hard item).
\subsection{The missing-information identity}
Let $I_N(\rho,\nu)$ denote the Fisher information of the single-frame count
$N$ with respect to $\log\lambda$, and $J = I_N/\nu$ its per-slot
normalization ($J = 1$ is the saturation ceiling: one unit of log-rate
information per dead-time slot). Writing $R_\nu \in [0,1)$ for the residual
dead-time phase left at the window end — the unobserved fraction of a
dead-time period truncated by the frame boundary — the information splits as
\begin{equation}
  I_N \;=\; \mathbb{E}[N] \;-\; \rho^{2}\,
  \mathbb{E}\bigl[\operatorname{Var}(R_\nu \mid N)\bigr].
  \label{eq:missing}
\end{equation}
The first term is the complete-data information of the underlying renewal
record (each registered event contributes one unit on the $\log\lambda$
scale); the second is the price of not observing the terminal phase: the
frame boundary censors the last dead-time period, and the count alone cannot
reveal how much of the window was spent in that final blind interval. The
terminal phase has $P(R_\nu = 0) = 1/(1+\rho)$ and density $\rho/(1+\rho)$
on $(0,1)$, so $\operatorname{Var}(R_\nu) \to \rho(\rho+4)/[12(1+\rho)^2]
\to 1/12$ at high load — the censoring price grows as $\rho^{2}/(12\nu)$
per slot.

\subsection{The cube-root law}
Expanding $J$ at large $\rho$ and large $\nu$,
\begin{equation}
  J(\rho,\nu) = 1 - \frac{1}{\rho} - \frac{\rho^{2}}{12\nu}
  + \frac{1}{\rho^{2}} - \frac{\rho}{6\nu} + \frac{\rho^{4}}{144\nu^{2}}
  + o(\nu^{-2/3}).
  \label{eq:Jexpansion}
\end{equation}
The leading tradeoff is between the saturation gap $1/\rho$ (a slot only
carries full rate information when occupied by a completed
detection--dead-time cycle) and the censoring price $\rho^{2}/(12\nu)$. The
marginal balance $1/\rho^{2} = \rho/(6\nu)$ gives $\rho^{3} = 6\nu$, i.e.\
the information-optimal load
\begin{equation}
  \rho^{*}(\nu) = (6\nu)^{1/3} - \tfrac{2}{3} + O(\nu^{-1/3}),
  \label{eq:cuberoot}
\end{equation}
with peak per-slot information
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  J(\rho^{*})
    &=1-\bigl(c^{-1}+c^{2}/12\bigr)\nu^{-1/3}+o(\nu^{-1/3})\\
    &=1-0.8255\,\nu^{-1/3}+o(\nu^{-1/3}),
    \qquad c=6^{1/3}.
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:peakJ}
\end{equation}
Exact evaluation of the renewal PMF gives $\rho^{*} = 4.53$, $6.16$, $7.87$,
$9.99$, $13.77$, $17.45$, and $22.16$ at $\nu = 20$, $50$, $100$, $200$,
$500$, $1000$, and $2000$, respectively; the closed form
$(6\nu)^{1/3} - 2/3$ gives $4.27$, $6.03$, $7.77$, $9.96$, $13.76$, $17.50$,
and $22.23$ — a $5.9\%$ gap at $\nu = 20$ shrinking to sub-percent by
$\nu = 500$, consistent with the $O(\nu^{-1/3})$ remainder
[Fig.~\ref{fig:ladder-mechanism}(a)].

Two structural remarks. (i) The ridge is not a count-variance pinning
effect: at $\rho = \rho^{*}$ the count variance
$\operatorname{Var} N = \nu^{1/3}/6^{2/3}$ still diverges with $\nu$; counts
pin to the ceiling only at the much larger scale $\rho \asymp \sqrt{\nu}$.
The information peak is an information-geometric tradeoff, not a saturation
artifact. (ii) Beyond the ridge ($\rho \ge \rho^{*}$) the count information
genuinely decreases — no estimator operating on counts, including the
exact-likelihood reference, recovers it. High-flux operation is therefore
beneficial only up to a computable boundary, and that boundary is one axis
of the operating map reported here.

\subsection{Deployment zoning and the CLT trust boundary}
The Gaussian (CLT) surrogate information satisfies $J_{\mathrm{exact}} /
J_{\mathrm{CLT}} \simeq 1 - \rho^{2}/(12\nu)$, so the surrogate stays within
$10\%$ of exact only for $\rho \le \rho_{0.9} \asymp \sqrt{1.2\,\nu}$ — a
$\sqrt{\nu}$ scaling, distinct from the $\nu^{1/3}$ ridge. We partition the
$(\rho,\nu)$ plane into an RQL deployment zone (per-pattern
$\rho_{95} \le 1$, the regime the campaigns demonstrate), a transition band,
an exact-reference mode (short-window and extreme-saturation reference
computations with the exact PMF), and the information-decreasing region
$\rho \ge \rho^{*}(\nu)$. Zoning uses the per-pattern 95th percentile of
$\rho$, not its mean: spread-occupancy patterns distribute per-frame load,
and the deployment guarantee must hold for the loaded tail, not the average.

\subsection{Relation to prior work}
% R12 frozen wording (issue #4), verbatim with our BibTeX keys. R12 access
% caveat: the Groenberg full text was paywalled for the checking agent;
% verdict "no ridge/identity found" at moderate-high confidence from the
% accessible record. Optional hardening: re-verify from a library PDF.
M\"uller systematized renewal-theoretic analysis of extended and
nonextended dead-time counters, relating distorted interarrival laws to
the resulting count statistics and measurement-origin
effects~\cite{muller1973deadtime}. Yu and Fessler derived exact first and
second moments for several dead-time counting models and used those
moments to study intensity correction and the adequacy of Poisson
reconstruction models~\cite{yu2000mean}. Wang et al.\ derived pileup
count statistics for idealized energy-discriminating nonparalyzable x-ray
detectors and evaluated task-specific Cram\'er--Rao performance as a
function of input rate, establishing substantial high-rate degradation of
spectral-counting advantages~\cite{wang2011pileup}. Alvarez used
renewal-process large-count moments and multivariate-Gaussian count
models to quantify pileup-induced covariance, signal-to-noise loss, and
material-estimation Cram\'er--Rao bounds~\cite{alvarez2014pileup}.
Task-specific finite-rate optima also arise in nuclear counting; for
example, B\'ecares and Bl\'azquez optimized counting rate by balancing
dead-time losses, counting uncertainty, source multiplicity, and
dead-time-calibration uncertainty~\cite{becares2012deadtime}. Gr\"onberg
et al.\ derived an exact finite-frame count probability mass function for
a seminonparalyzable detector with nonzero pulse length, validated its
moments against an electronic Monte Carlo model, and used the exact
distribution together with Poisson and Gaussian approximations to
evaluate Fisher information for a single-threshold soft-tissue-density
task as a function of count rate; their fixed-frame Fisher curve exhibits
an interior maximum followed by high-rate
decline~\cite{gronberg2018deadtime}.

A complementary line retains richer event information. Rapp et al.\
modeled the sequence of dead-time-distorted detection times as a Markov
chain, enabling accurate high-flux single-photon lidar and large
acquisition-time reductions~\cite{rapp2021highflux}. Jorgensen and
Johnson derived asymptotic Fisher-information rates and efficient
estimators for periodic dead-time event-detection processes, showing that
activation and event statistics can retain information discarded by
conventional histogram summaries~\cite{jorgensen2026deadtime}.

Here we isolate a different detector-channel question:\ an ideal
nonparalyzable detector is active at the start of a finite exposure, only
its integrated scalar count is retained, and the parameter of interest is
the log arrival rate itself. Treating $\rho = \lambda\tau$ and
$\nu = T/\tau$ as independent axes, we derive the exact finite-window
information surface, the terminal-phase missing-information identity of
Eq.~(\ref{eq:missing}), and the principal information ridge of
Eq.~(\ref{eq:cuberoot}). These statements concern the active-start ideal
count-only channel and do not replace task-specific finite-rate optima
for nonzero-pulse-length detectors or information bounds for
time-resolved dead-time event records.

\section{Study 1: dense patterns --- the multiplex-limited regime}
\label{sec:study1}
% Round-8 §Q3: Study 1 in MAIN TEXT before Study 2 — its frozen negative and

\subsection{Preregistered design}
We tested whether renewal quasi-likelihood reconstruction permits operation
at $\rhobar = 0.6$ with a statistically significant reduction in total
optical integration time relative to the conventional $\rhobar = 0.05$
operating point, on $64^{2}$ scenes under dense Bernoulli illumination
(occupancy $50\%$). Scenes are the units of inference:\ 24 confirmatory
detail targets (six structural families $\times$ four instances), each
measured at nine dwell points $\nu \in \{5,\dots,2000\}$ with five
measurement seeds. Per scene, seed-mean radiometric PSNR versus optical
integration time is isotonically regressed for each arm; the quality
target is $Q_{90}$, the safe-arm minimum plus $90\%$ of the safe-arm span,
and the acquisition-speed ratio $S_j$ is the ratio of safe to high-flux
crossing times, with a frozen censoring taxonomy (a safe-arm span below
$0.5$~dB is uninformative; a high-flux arm that never reaches $Q_{90}$
scores $S_j = 0$). The confirmatory gate --- median $S \ge 3$,
family-stratified nested-bootstrap ($10\,000$ replicates) lower bound
$> 1$, and $\ge 18/24$ scenes with $S_j > 1$ --- and every constant above
were frozen in an immutable commit before any confirmatory reconstruction
existed, together with a fixed-dwell secondary endpoint (median
$\ge 1$~dB at the terminal dwell, lower bound $> 0$, $\ge 18/24$
positive).

\subsection{Preregistered outcome}
The confirmatory time-to-quality gate returned negative:\ in $24/24$ scenes
the safe-arm quality span across the entire dwell grid fell below the
preregistered $0.5$~dB informativeness floor
(\textsc{safe\_range\_uninformative}; $S_{\mathrm{gate}} = 1$ by the frozen
censoring taxonomy), so no time-to-quality acceleration could be certified.
The preregistered fixed-dwell secondary, in contrast, was uniformly
positive but small: at $\nu = 2000$ the high-flux arm gained a median
$+0.28$~dB (radiometric PSNR), positive in $24/24$ scenes, with
family-stratified bootstrap lower bound $+0.23$~dB --- statistically
significant, but far below the preregistered $1$~dB practical-relevance
bar. Both outcomes are reported as frozen; no endpoint, gate, or analysis
constant was altered after the freeze.

\subsection{Natural-scene cohort (secondary, no gate)}
% Frozen analyzer output (results/round63_s2_natural, cohort=natural,
% gate-null per round-7 §4; R9 Q5 §3 main-text scope).
On the preregistered natural-image cohort (30 scenes, same dense-pattern
protocol, no confirmatory gate), the acquisition-speed endpoint was
safe-range-uninformative for $12/30$ scenes, while the fixed-dwell gain was
larger and more uniform than on the detail cohort:\ median $+1.44$~dB,
$29/30$ scenes positive, family-stratified bootstrap lower bound
$+1.18$~dB. For context, classical correlation ghost imaging on the
identical measurement records scores $4.8$--$6.3$~dB --- at this photon
budget ($\approx 95$ detected photons per pixel over the full
acquisition) the likelihood-based reconstruction, not the sampling ratio,
is what renders the data usable. Smooth, prior-limited scenes thus occupy the low-benefit
region of the acquisition-speed map even where the fixed-dwell radiometric
benefit of high-flux operation is substantial.

\subsection{Mechanism: bucket multiplex contrast}
The two outcomes are jointly explained by the bucket contrast of dense
multiplexed illumination. For occupancy-$1/2$ patterns on $n$ pixels the
bucket energies $u_i = a_i^{\top}x$ concentrate as
$\Cu = \mathrm{sd}(u)/\mathrm{mean}(u) \sim 1/\sqrt{n}$
($\approx 1.6\%$ at $64^{2}$): the structural information rides on a
relative modulation of order $10^{-2}$, so the per-count information about
\emph{structure} is contrast-limited, not count-limited --- raising the flux
adds photons to a nearly constant bucket. This is the multiplex-limited
regime of the operating map; it motivates Study~2.

\section{Study 2: the contrast ladder --- equal load, equal dose}
\label{sec:study2}
\subsection{Preregistered design}
% Round-8 frozen protocol, summarized; full spec in docs/ROUND63_SPEC_D2.md
% + ROUND63_GPT_ROUND8_RULING.md (freeze tag study2-freeze).
Study~2 varies spatial occupancy $k \in \{512, 32, 16, 1\}$ (occupancy
$50\%$, $3.1\%$, $1.6\%$, $0.1\%$ of $n = 1024$ pixels at $32^{2}$) using
binary matrices with exact row and column sums $k$, while holding the
\emph{mean detector load and the incident photon dose fixed across the
ladder}: the reconstruction operator is $A^{(k)} = (n/k)\,B^{(k)}$ at flux
$\Phi = \rhobar/\tau$, so every occupancy shares identical mean count rate,
identical incident photons per pattern, and identical cumulative dose per
object pixel; only the on-pixel peak intensity rises as $n/k$. Sparse
patterns therefore cannot win by starving the detector --- the mechanism
prior sparse-pattern work uses to mitigate pile-up
\cite{liu2019pileup,han2025sparsehadamard}. Only the $k = 16$ arm carries the confirmatory
gate (constants unchanged from Study~1); $k = 32$ is a preregistered
robustness arm and $k = 512$ and $k = 1$ are controls, all without gates.
Two mechanism variables are recorded descriptively and never enter any
gate:\ the measured bucket-energy contrast $\Cu = \mathrm{sd}_i(u_i) /
\mathrm{mean}_i(u_i)$ and the contrast-to-count-noise index $\Gamma =
\Cu\sqrt{\nu\rho/(1+\rho)}$, whose crossing of unity marks the onset of
the photon-limited structural regime. A contrast-to-noise ratio on
generator-frozen signal/background regions is carried as a no-gate
co-secondary, and all photon budgets are reported on two axes:\ the
measured-photons axis $S_{\mathrm{det}} = (Mn)^{-1}\sum_i N_i$ (the
convention of Ref.~\cite{hao2025photonlimited}, whose experiments span
$S_{\mathrm{det}} = 0.096$--$0.44$) and the incident axis
$S_{\mathrm{inc}}$, which differ nonlinearly under dead time.

\subsection{Preregistered verdicts}
The preregistered Study-2 primary endpoint was formally negative because its breadth criterion failed: 16 of 24 scenes accelerated, below the required 18 of 24.
The two effect-size criteria passed, with median
$S_{\mathrm{gate}}=6.796$ and family-stratified bootstrap lower bound $2.835$,
but they do not rescue the failed primary. The preregistered fixed-dwell
secondary passed: median $+1.16$~dB at the terminal dwell, 19 of 24 scenes
positive, and lower bound $+0.72$~dB. The frozen hierarchy is therefore
``primary formally negative; fixed-dwell secondary passed,'' with no endpoint,
geometry, or image-class redesign.

The eight non-accelerating scenes comprise the entire microtexture family
($0/4$), three of four contour instances, and one chirp instance, whereas
glyph, maze, and spokes accelerated $4/4$. After unblinding, a descriptive
family-level analysis showed that the six success fractions were monotonically
ordered by their mean measured bucket contrasts: maze $\Cu=0.53$ ($4/4$),
glyph $0.41$ ($4/4$), spokes $0.38$ ($4/4$), chirp $0.28$ ($3/4$), contour
$0.27$ ($1/4$), and microtexture $0.17$ ($0/4$). This ordering was not
preregistered, did not enter any decision rule, and does not estimate a
universal critical contrast.

Across the preregistered no-gate occupancy controls, fixed-dwell gain was
nonmonotone and largest among the tested rungs at $k=32$ ($3.1\%$ occupancy),
where the median was $+4.13$~dB. This is evidence of a
contrast--conditioning trade-off, not an ``optimal sparsity'' claim. Complete
per-rung and per-scene values are given in the supplement.

\subsection{Contrast and conditioning across occupancies}
The measured bucket contrast follows the designed ladder: $\Cu=0.040$,
$0.222$, $0.316$, and $1.270$ at $k=512$, $32$, $16$, and $1$,
respectively. At $\rhobar=0.6$ and $\nu=2000$, the corresponding descriptive
contrast-to-count-noise index is $\Gamma=1.09$, $6.07$, $8.66$, and $34.79$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:ladder-mechanism}). Sparsity increases bucket modulation but
also raises the conditioning burden $\kappa_A=n/k$; the competing trends
explain why fixed-dwell benefit is nonmonotone rather than increasing steadily
with contrast. The dense-rung value remains close to the Study-1 magnitude,
showing that the change from $64^2$ to $32^2$ did not manufacture the effect.

\subsection{Mechanism separation: equal-load gain is not rate starvation}
% Numbers: separation CSVs (commit b0a70ad). The closest-prior killer
% (round-8 §13): prior sparse-SPI mitigates pile-up by REDUCING count rate.
On the six frozen mechanism representatives we ran the $k = 16$ geometry in
two flux conventions:\ (i) \emph{normalized} --- the Study-2 mechanism, equal
mean load and equal dose; and (ii) \emph{unnormalized} --- sparsity is
allowed to reduce the detector count rate, as in prior sparse-pattern
mitigation. In the normalized convention the high-flux arm retains a
positive gain of $+1.81$ and $+1.10$~dB at $\nu = 200$ and $2000$,
respectively, at detector loads of $S_{\mathrm{det}} = 0.072$--$0.72$
counts per pattern per pixel. The unnormalized convention shows larger
\emph{relative} deltas but strictly lower absolute quality at every dwell
(e.g.\ $7.45$ versus $10.26$~dB at the safe point, $\nu = 2000$): it wins
its comparison by starving the detector. The equal-load, equal-dose gain is
therefore a contrast effect, not a rate-reduction effect.

\subsection{Dynamic scattering}
% Numbers: scattering CSVs (commit b0a70ad); Fig (f) in S_det units.
Under multiplicative dynamic scattering (lognormal AR(1) factors,
$\mathrm{CV}(\alpha) = 0.20$, applied before dead time) the high-flux gain
persists and grows with dwell:\ $+0.66$, $+1.84$, and $+2.32$~dB at
$\nu = 20$, $200$, and $2000$, respectively (Supplementary Information),
reported on the measured-photons axis $S_{\mathrm{det}}$ for direct
comparison with single-photon underwater single-pixel experiments
\cite{hao2025photonlimited}.

\subsection{Robustness to detector mismatch}
\label{sec:mismatch}
% Descriptive (R9: no late robustness gate). Numbers:
% results/round63_s2_s3/S3_ANCHORS.md, paired vs the no-mismatch baseline.
Detector-mismatch consequences were quantified descriptively on the
$64^{2}$ cohort at the preregistered anchor ($M/n = 0.5$, $\nu = 500$),
pairing each perturbed cell with its no-mismatch baseline. Every anchor
perturbation leaves the RQL radiometric PSNR within $0.34$~dB of
baseline at both operating points:\ dead-time miscalibration
$\hat\tau = \tau(1 \pm 0.1)$ costs at most $0.12$~dB (with a
$\pm 6\%$ radiometric flux bias, the largest observed); a $10\%$ dark
rate costs $0.16$~dB when calibrated and $0.34$~dB when unmodeled;
afterpulsing at $2$--$5\%$ costs at most $0.09$~dB. Under the same
perturbations the pre-correction arm degrades more in five of six
anchors (up to $0.79$~dB, unmodeled dark). Start-mode, guard-time, and
dead-time-jitter axes, severity sweeps to $50\%$ dark, and the
descriptive measurement-audit distributions are tabulated in the
supplement.

\subsection{Exact-likelihood reference at small scale}
% Frozen S4(ii) grid (spec D2); results/round63_s4 (864 paired cells).
Because RQL is a convex surrogate, we verified at $8^{2}$ and $16^{2}$
that it tracks the exact renewal likelihood under an identical TV
functional and the identical frozen selection rule:\ across the full
preregistered grid ($\rho \in \{0.03,\dots,2\}$, $\nu \in \{20,\dots,2000\}$,
three seeds, six scenes, 864 paired reconstructions) the median
exact-minus-RQL radiometric-PSNR gap is $\le 0.01$~dB throughout the
deployment zone and reaches only $-0.05$~dB in the extreme-load corner
($16^{2}$, $\rho \ge 0.6$, $\nu = 2000$). Individual cells differ in both
directions (up to $9$~dB), consistent with the nonconvexity of the exact
objective, with no systematic advantage to either arm; full tables and
multistart diagnostics are provided in the supplement. The convex
surrogate therefore tracked the exact reference closely on this grid. Under
the frozen TV settings,
the exact and approximate likelihood reconstructions agreed to the stated
numerical tolerance, and the exact calculation added little runtime at those
tested sizes. This empirical agreement does not establish uniqueness of the
TV-regularized solution; convexity of the data term alone is insufficient when
the design is underdetermined and the TV penalty is not strictly convex.

\section{Discussion}
% Frozen wordings: R9 Q5 §5 (position), round-8 §Q3
% (peak irradiance), R9 Q2 §3 / Q3 §3 / Q4 §5 (future-work paragraphs,
% verbatim).
The scientific position established by the two studies is deliberately
mixed. Dense illumination is multiplex-limited; sparse equal-load
illumination produces large and statistically supported benefits for many,
but not sufficiently many, preregistered scenes to pass the breadth gate.
The scene response is nevertheless structured by bucket contrast, while
the tested occupancy ladder exposes an independent conditioning penalty.
Together with the exact count-information ridge and the passed fixed-dwell
secondary, this constitutes an operating map:\ a computable answer to when
high-flux operation is worth engaging, rather than a universal
acceleration claim.

\subsection{Limitations}
Three scope boundaries must be stated plainly. First, all results are
simulation-based; the exact renewal forward model, detector-mismatch envelopes,
and frozen benchmark do not substitute for optical-bench validation. Second,
sparse operation at equal dose concentrates the same illumination into fewer
pixels: the on-pixel peak irradiance rises as $n/k$ ($64\times$ at the
gate-carrying occupancy), so source power, spatial-light-modulator limits,
laser safety, or local photodamage may bind before the detector does. Third,
the operating map assumes a calibrated, stable detector dead time. Jitter,
drift, correlation, or paralyzable recovery changes the count-information
surface; deployment therefore requires recovery-time calibration and a
detector-specific load limit. The reported sparse rungs are conditional on
both peak-power headroom and that calibration.

Three extensions follow directly from the failure structure. For
uniformly scattered fixed-$k$ supports,
$\Cu^{2} = \frac{n-k}{k(n-1)}\,(n\lVert x\rVert_2^{2} - 1)$, giving the
observed $k^{-1/2}$ spatial-averaging law. A local support instead
averages only the effective number of independent correlation cells
within the patch and may therefore preserve modulation from scale-matched
structure. Local, foveated and multiscale single-pixel patterns have
previously been developed for resolution and measurement efficiency;
optimizing their spatial scale for equal-load dead-time contrast is a
distinct future design problem.

The present results do not establish that adaptive illumination is
necessary. Although dead-time nonlinearity makes the statistically
efficient pattern scale scene-dependent, a strict adaptive advantage
requires a specified scene class, resource constraint and loss. A natural
follow-up is to seek a minimax or Chernoff-information separation between
fixed multiscale illumination and a two-stage
scale-estimation-plus-matched-pattern policy.

A coherent extension would use a coarse complex-field estimate to
synthesize an optical displacement that suppresses the predicted common
mode before photon counting. Unlike electronic balanced subtraction, a
dark-port receiver removes photons before dead-time loss. Exact nulling,
however, gives quadratic residual sensitivity and demands spatial-mode
matching and phase stability; the relevant operating variable is
therefore an optimized displacement bias rather than maximal null depth.
Testing this receiver first under static and then dynamic scattering is a
natural optical-bench extension. More broadly, architectures that retain
per-photon timing \cite{rapp2019deadtime,rapp2021highflux} occupy a different
information regime than the count-only channel analyzed here; the map
presented in this work is the count-only baseline against which such
richer architectures can be costed. The proposed operating map, the
deployment-legal reconstruction rule, and the frozen open benchmark open
up an avenue for dwell-time-limited and photon-budget-limited
single-pixel systems operating deep in the dead-time regime.
